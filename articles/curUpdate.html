THE INTRODUCTION OF THE MWK 5000 NOTE AND ITS IMPLICATIONS FOR MALAWI’S ECONOMY



INTRODUCTION

On the 23rd of November 2021, the Reserve Bank of Malawi announced its intention to introduce a 5000 Malawi Kwacha banknote and to upgrade the pre-existing 2000 Kwacha banknote. These actions are to be implemented on the 24th of February, 2022 (Kalumbi, 2021). This announcement was met with concern from the general public. While there is much to be concerned about in the larger economic climate, it is important to address these concerns and reassure the public. This is because an environment of economic anxiety is one that breeds misinformation and consumer behavior that ultimately harms those engaging in it. Thus, the purpose of this document is to explain the purpose of the MK 5000 note and its implications for Malawi’s natural economy.


PUBLIC OUTCRY AGAINST HIGHER CURRENCY NOTES

There was a prevalent assumption that the introduction of this currency denomination would lead to unsustainable levels of inflation. This is a fear that has been documented in several other countries where larger denominations were introduced. These include Ghana, Nigeria and Thailand (Franses, 2006). Additionally, it has been assumed, in countries like Poland, that the introduction of a new denomination is an attempt by the government to hide evidence of corruption, money laundering and similar crimes (Manikowski, 2017).
These concerns are, however, unfounded. It has been empirically observed that the introduction of new banknotes does not result in inflation nor does not indicate that there will be inflation in the future (Franses, 2006).
At worst, the announcement of a higher denomination may create the public perception that inflation will take place if the citizens of the particular country are not told otherwise. This is because consumer behavior, and by extension consumer panic, is a determinant of inflation. It is important to note that long term inflation resulting from a redenomination announcement or the introduction of a higher currency denomination has not been empirically observed (Egbuna & Obikili, 2013).

UNDER WHAT CONDITIONS ARE HIGHER DENOMINATIONS INTRODUCED

One role of Monetary Authorities and Central Banks is to provide banknotes and coins to meet the demand of consumers. Monetary Authorities fulfil this role by regularly reevaluating the currency in circulation based on several criteria. On average, countries reevaluate their currencies and introduce higher denominations every 10 years. Banknotes need to remain resistant to forgery in the presence of the current technological conditions and the level of criminal activity in the country. Banknotes need to be made of a durable and low cost material, based on the available cost and technology. The criteria most important for this document is that bank notes and coins in circulation need to have an optimal denomination structure. (Manikowski, 2017).
An optimal denomination structure means that the different denominations of coins and banknotes in circulation must be in the best combination to facilitate the most efficient transactions. A practical example of this, is the one Kwacha coin. While this coin is currently in circulation, it is rarely used in transactions because its value is very small compared to the goods and services on the market. If one decided to pay for even a small purchase (a cake worth MK100), it would still be an inefficient transaction. This is a symptom of a currency without an optimal denomination.
Banknotes organically become less optimal over time because of socioeconomic factors that have led to goods in the economy having an increased value over time. This is for a combination of two reasons. First of all, naturally occurring inflation raises the price of all goods and services in the economy. The currency denomination needs to increase along with this, otherwise banknotes will be too small for efficient circumstances. Secondly economic expansion leads to the introduction of new and more expensive goods into the economy. An example of this is the presence of cars in the market that were not available in previous years (Ghana, 2019). The optimization of a currency denomination is based on two criteria:

  • 1. Does it minimize the cost of money?

    • The cost of money, in this context, has two references. The first is the cost on the Central Bank, RBM. When a currency denomination is too low, it takes more notes to make a transaction. Because of this, the Central Bank has to issue more currency, a process that costs taxpayers money (Pattanarangsun, 2011).
  • 2. Does it make cash transactions more efficiently?

    • This is the cost faced by stakeholders; including, consumers, retailers and commercial banks. When carrying out transactions, there is an associated cost. There are several examples of such costs. The first is the physical inconvenience of carrying a large number of coins or banknotes for a simple transaction. This is also the difficulty of counting out large amounts of banknotes and the potential for theft and mistakes within these transactions. Additionally, this is represented by a loss in the demand for banknotes compared to digital transactions. While digital transactions are innovative, facilitating them comes with a fee, unlike cash payments.
    These costs are made worse by a sub-optimal denomination structure. This is because, payments made with an unnecessarily large number of coins and notes complicate the transaction. An example of this is the inconvenience of counting out the price of a restaurant meal using a stack of 20 kwacha notes. This also reduces the demand for the currency compared to an alternative one.

WHY INTRODUCE HIGHER DENOMINATIONS IN A DIGITALIZING WORLD

While digital monetization represents the future, at present, cash is still one of the most common forms of exchange in the country (RBM, 2022). This is particularly noted in rural areas and in the informal sector which the financial sector has not yet penetrated. Cash transactions are also cheaper to facilitate than mobile banking and other digital transactions (Ghana, 2019).

OUTCOMES OF INTRODUCING HIGHER DENOMINATIONS

According to the empirical theory, there are three outcomes of introducing a higher denomination to a currency:

  • 1. A reduction in real demand for money
    • In this case, fewer banknotes will be necessary to facilitate a transaction. This will reduce the demand for banknotes and increase the efficiency of transactions. This is known as the efficiency effect. While this places upwards pressure on price levels and may cause them to rise, it is not enough to create inflation. (Suggestion: how about “it is not equivalent to inflation”?)
  • 2. A demand for real cash relative to digital money
    • The demand for banknotes, as opposed to digital transactions, will increase because the inconvenience associated with physical transactions will be reduced. This is known as the substitution effect which will reduce the pressure on the price level. This also reduces the demand for assets that gain interest. This increases the real balance to income ratio and reduces the opportunity for inflation. This will also reduce the money multiplier effect and reduce the nominal stock of money, reducing the pressure on the price level. (as an economics layman I don’t know the meaning of nominal)
  • 3. An increase in the real flow of income
    • This means that labor and real resources will be saved after the efficiency of transactions is increased. For example, less time will be spent counting money, fewer banknotes will be physically transported and fewer notes will need to be issued. This represents an increase in the demand for money (Egbuna & Obikili, 2013).

HAPPENED

The (then) Governor of the Reserve Bank of Malawi, Dr. Wilson Banda, explained much of the decision making behind the establishment of the MK5000 banknote. He stated that according to the RBM Currency Management Policy, high banknotes should not account for more than 60 percent of the total value of currency. He then stated that, currently, MK2000 kwacha notes exceed over 80 percent of the currency in circulation. This implies that the Malawi Kwacha has a suboptimal denomination structure. All of the banknotes and coins worth less than MK2000 are so inefficient that there is a massive demand for MK2000 notes. Rather than remain with this system, it is sensible to introduce a higher denomination.
Additionally, the Governor stated that new MK2000 notes with more security features will be issued alongside the MK5000 notes. This is because this note, being the most in-demand currency, is uniquely vulnerable to fabrication.
Thus, the RBM is being proactive in addressing this currency issue and the decision to undertake this will not result in inflation. While the entire operation will cost 2.8 billion Kwacha, this is less money in the long run than what it would cost to issue small banknotes that are not in demand and lead to inefficient transactions.

CONCLUSION

Pretend you are a shopkeeper at a toy store. Your supervisor has new items and among them is a Lego set. Not one of those trash new sets where its attached to a movie franchise and the model has already been built for you. No. It’s the deluxe classical XXL box of Legos (1,500 pieces!) in all the colors. You are in awe of its beauty. A ten-year-old child approaches you, at the counter. She has a suitcase with her. You smile your best customer service smile. She points at the Lego set. You tell her it’s worth MWK 80,000 . You wonder where her parents are. Wordlessly, she opens the suitcase and to your horror, its filled with neatly stacked MWK50 notes. She informs you that its worth MWK 80,000. A part of your screaming mind wonders where she got all that money. You desperately want to take her word for it but your supervisor is eyeballing you from across the room. Dejectedly, you begin to count. This is one of the many challenges faces by people who exist within an economy where the currency has an inefficient denomination. Inefficient currency denomination can lead to miscounting, fraud, theft, difficulty in transportation and general frustration. Thus, when a country introduces a new and higher currency, it means that this problem has existed for some time and the monetary authority is correctly taking responsibility.
